A growing recognition of ADHD

One of the most prevalent neurodevelopmental diseases is ADHD. It frequently persists into maturity and is initially diagnosed in childhood. Children with ADHD may struggle to focus, manage impulsive behaviors, or exhibit excessive activity. Although it was often believed that ADHD only affected a small percentage of children, it is now generally acknowledged that it can affect people of all ages, even as adults.
Approximately 5% of school-age children and 2.5% of adults suffer from ADHD.

Typical ADHD Symptoms

Those with ADHD frequently exhibit high levels of symptoms including impulsivity, hyperactivity, poor focus, disorganization, and emotional instability.

Types Of ADHD

Recent Research And News


The first description of attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) dates back to 1902, and the disorder is still being studied today.
Numerous studies have looked into the causes, symptoms, interventions, and effects of ADHD on the lives of affected persons. The following are some recent study topics:

Power of Exercise

According to a new analysis of research, the best non-drug strategy for enhancing mental abilities frequently impacted by ADHD is physical activity.

Genetics

The goal of ongoing research into the genetics of ADHD is to
pinpoint particular genes or genetic variants that may predispose people to the illness. New research has shown many genes linked to ADHD, providing insight into the genetic aspects of the disorder.

Dementia

According to a recent study, adults with ADHD were almost three times more likely than adults without ADHD to have an increased
risk of dementia.

Environmental Factors

Recent research looks into how environmental factors like toxins during pregnancy, maternal smoking, or early childhood experiences might contribute to ADHD. By understanding how these factors interact with genetics, scientists hope to find ways to prevent and treat ADHD more effectively.
